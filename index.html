What are Altcoins?

"Altcoin" = "Alternative Coin" — any cryptocurrency other than Bitcoin.

There are over 10,000 cryptocurrencies, but only a small percentage have significant real-world adoption.

Why were Altcoins created?

To improve Bitcoin or solve other problems:

  • Smart contracts
  • Faster transactions
  • Lower fees
  • Decentralized applications (dApps)
  • DeFi, Gaming, NFTs
  • Cross-border payments

Each cryptocurrency has its own purpose. Click the tabs above to explore them.

ETH

Ethereum (ETH)

The world's decentralized computer

Ethereum is the second-largest cryptocurrency and much more than just a cryptocurrency — it's a platform where developers can build applications without a central authority.

Smart Contracts

Self-executing programs on the blockchain. Example: "If payment received → transfer ownership." No bank or lawyer needed.

Ethereum Powers:

  • Decentralized Finance (DeFi)
  • NFT marketplaces
  • Blockchain games
  • Lending platforms
  • Decentralized exchanges
  • Thousands of dApps

Advantages

  • Huge developer community
  • Most used for smart contracts
  • Constant technological improvements
  • Large ecosystem

Challenges

  • Gas Fees can be expensive
  • Transactions sometimes slower
$

Stablecoins

Stability in a volatile market

The biggest challenge in crypto is price volatility. Bitcoin can move several percent per day.

Stablecoins are designed to maintain a stable value, usually pegged to the US dollar:

1 USDT ≈ 1 USD | 1 USDC ≈ 1 USD

Why do people use stablecoins?

  • Preserve value during market volatility
  • Send money across borders quickly
  • Trade between cryptos without a bank
  • Liquidity in DeFi

USDT (Tether)

  • Most used stablecoin
  • Available on nearly every exchange
  • Highest trading volume

USDC (USD Coin)

  • Transparency and compliance
  • Often preferred by institutions
  • Regulatory focus
SOL

Solana (SOL)

Speed and low costs

Solana is a blockchain designed for high speed and low transaction costs. It can process thousands of transactions per second.

Popular applications:

  • NFT marketplaces
  • Blockchain gaming
  • Payment systems
  • DeFi applications

Advantages

  • Very fast transactions
  • Low fees
  • Growing ecosystem

Challenges

  • Historical network outages
  • Reliability improving
XRP

XRP

International payments

XRP was developed to improve international money transfers. Instead of waiting days, XRP settles payments in seconds.

Advantages

  • Fast settlement
  • Low transaction costs
  • Focus on cross-border payments

Note

  • More centralized structure
  • Regulatory uncertainty
ADA

Cardano (ADA)

Scientifically grounded development

Cardano focuses on security, research, and sustainability. Development is based on academic research and peer-reviewed studies.

Advantages

  • Strong focus on security
  • Energy-efficient design
  • Long-term development approach

Challenges

  • Slower development
  • Fewer dApps than Ethereum
BNB

BNB

The heart of the BNB Chain ecosystem

BNB is the native cryptocurrency of the BNB Chain ecosystem. Originally created for discounts on Binance trading fees.

Uses:

  • Paying transaction fees
  • DeFi
  • NFT marketplaces
  • Blockchain games
  • Smart contracts

Choosing the Right Cryptocurrency

Ask yourself these questions:

  • Do I want long-term exposure to an established digital asset?
  • Am I interested in decentralized applications?
  • Do I need fast and cheap transactions?
  • Am I looking for a stable digital asset for transfers?

There is no "best" cryptocurrency — each has strengths depending on the use case.

Common Beginner Mistakes

Avoid these pitfalls:

  • Treating all cryptocurrencies as the same
  • Investing without understanding the purpose
  • Chasing hype or social media trends
  • Ignoring market risk and volatility
  • Not diversifying

Key Takeaways

  • Stablecoins = stable value for payments & trading
  • Solana = speed & low costs
  • XRP = fast international payments
  • Cardano = security & research-based
  • BNB = broad blockchain ecosystem

Understanding the purpose of each cryptocurrency helps you evaluate projects more effectively.

What is Technical Analysis?

Technical Analysis (TA) is the study of historical price movements and trading volume to help traders identify trends, potential entry/exit points, and overall market sentiment.

Important Note

TA does not predict the future with certainty. It provides tools for evaluating probabilities.

Core Principles of Technical Analysis:

1. The market discounts everything

Price reflects all known information, news, and market expectations.

2. Prices move in trends

Markets often move in the same direction until conditions change.

3. History repeats itself

Patterns formed by buyers and sellers can reappear under similar conditions.

Beginner

Candlestick Charts

Candlestick charts are the most commonly used price charts in cryptocurrency trading. Each candlestick represents the price movement over a chosen time period.

Four key values:

  • Open — Price at the start of the period
  • Close — Price at the end of the period
  • High — Highest price reached
  • Low — Lowest price reached
Bullish

Close > Open

Bearish

Close < Open

Bullish Candle: Closing price is higher than opening price. Indicates buying pressure.

Bearish Candle: Closing price is lower than opening price. Indicates selling pressure.

Beginner

Support & Resistance

These are two of the most important concepts in technical analysis.

Support

A price level where buying interest was strong enough to stop or slow a decline.

Example: Bitcoin repeatedly falls to $100,000 and buyers step in.

Resistance

A price level where selling pressure was strong enough to stop or slow a rise.

Example: Bitcoin repeatedly struggles to stay above $110,000.

Key Insight

When support is broken, it can often become resistance (and vice versa). This is called a "role reversal".

Beginner

Trend Analysis

Markets generally move in one of three directions:

Uptrend

  • Higher highs
  • Higher lows
  • Indicates buyers in control

Downtrend

  • Lower highs
  • Lower lows
  • Indicates sellers in control

Sideways (Range)

Price moves between support and resistance without a clear up or down trend.

Beginner

Trading Volume

Volume measures how much of an asset was traded during a specific period.

Why Volume Matters:

Higher volume on a price move = stronger market participation and more reliability.

Lower volume on a price move = weaker conviction, possibly less reliable.

Volume Confirmation

A price increase with increasing volume is seen as stronger than a rise with decreasing volume.

Intermediate

Moving Averages (MA)

A Moving Average smoothes price data to identify the overall trend.

Common Types:

SMA (Simple Moving Average)

Average price over a fixed number of periods. Simple and stable.

EMA (Exponential Moving Average)

Gives more weight to recent prices. Reacts faster to market changes.

Popular Settings:

20 EMA — Short-term trend

50 EMA — Medium-term trend

100 EMA — Longer-term trend

200 EMA — Long-term trend indicator

Important

Many traders look at the 200-period moving average as a long-term trend indicator, but always use it together with other tools.

Intermediate

Relative Strength Index (RSI)

RSI measures the speed and magnitude of recent price movements. The indicator ranges from 0 to 100.

RSI = 0-30 → Possibly OVERSOLD

RSI = 30-70 → Neutral zone

RSI = 70-100 → Possibly OVERBOUGHT

Caution!

These are not automatic buy or sell signals. In strong trends, RSI can stay above 70 or below 30 for a long time.

Practical Tip

Use RSI in combination with support/resistance levels and trend analysis for better results.

Intermediate

MACD

MACD stands for Moving Average Convergence Divergence. It compares two moving averages to identify changes in momentum.

MACD Consists Of:

  • MACD Line — The difference between two EMAs
  • Signal Line — EMA of the MACD Line
  • Histogram — Visual representation of the difference

Possible Interpretations:

MACD crosses above Signal Line → Possibly increasing bullish momentum

MACD crosses below Signal Line → Possibly increasing bearish momentum

Always Combine

MACD works best in combination with other analysis tools. Never use one indicator by itself.

Intermediate

Bollinger Bands

Bollinger Bands consist of three lines that help visualize market volatility:

  • Upper Band — Upper boundary
  • Middle Moving Average — Middle line (usually 20 SMA)
  • Lower Band — Lower boundary

Wide Bands

High volatility. Market is active. Possibly preparing for a large move.

Narrow Bands

Low volatility. Can sometimes precede larger price moves ("squeeze").

Advanced

Fibonacci Retracement

Fibonacci Retracement is a tool to identify areas where price might find support or resistance during a pullback.

Common Retracement Levels:

23.6% — Weak retracement

38.2% — Mild retracement

50.0% — Midpoint (not a true Fibonacci, but popular)

61.8% — "Golden Ratio" — strong level

78.6% — Deep retracement

Guidelines, Not Guarantees

These levels are guidelines rather than guarantees. Price does not always react exactly at these levels.

Intermediate

Chart Patterns

Recognizable patterns that help traders identify potential trend changes:

Double Top

Two peaks at about the same level. Often seen as a sign of weakening upward momentum.

Double Bottom

Two troughs at about the same level. Often seen as a sign of weakening downward momentum.

Head and Shoulders

Three peaks with the middle one highest. Can signal a potential trend reversal.

Ascending Triangle

Continuation pattern. Higher lows with flat resistance. Can indicate increasing buying pressure.

Descending Triangle

Continuation pattern. Lower highs with flat support. Can indicate increasing selling pressure.

Beginner

Risk Management

Technical analysis is most effective when combined with disciplined risk management.

Good Practices:

  • Risk per trade — Never risk more than a small percentage of your capital on one trade.
  • Stop-loss orders — Use where appropriate to limit losses.
  • Diversification — Spread your funds across multiple assets.
  • Avoid emotional decisions — Follow your plan, not your emotions.
  • Accept losses — Losses are part of trading. Learn from them.
Beginner

Trading Psychology

Markets are driven not only by data but also by human emotions.

Common Psychological Pitfalls:

  • FOMO (Fear of Missing Out) — Entering trades because others are buying.
  • Panic Selling — Selling during sharp drops without a plan.
  • Overconfidence — Thinking successful trades guarantee future success.
  • Revenge Trading — Taking impulsive trades after a loss.

Successful Traders

Follow a trading plan instead of reacting emotionally. Discipline is more important than any single indicator.

Key Takeaway

Technical analysis is a decision-support tool, not a prediction system. Experienced traders typically combine:

  • Price action
  • Support and resistance
  • Trend analysis
  • Volume
  • Multiple indicators
  • Risk management
  • Fundamental analysis

Learning to interpret these tools together — and understanding that no indicator is always accurate — is more valuable than relying on a single signal.

What is a Technical Indicator?

A technical indicator is a mathematical calculation based on historical market data, such as price and volume.

Indicators help answer:

  • Is the market in a trend?
  • Is momentum increasing or decreasing?
  • Is the market overbought or oversold?
  • How volatile is the market?

Four Categories of Indicators:

Trend Indicators

Show the direction of the market. Example: Moving Averages

Momentum Indicators

Measure the strength of price movements. Example: RSI, MACD

Volume Indicators

Analyze buying and selling activity. Example: OBV

Volatility Indicators

Measure how much prices fluctuate. Example: Bollinger Bands, ATR

Important Warning

No indicator guarantees future price movements. They are most effective when used together with price action, volume, and solid risk management.

Momentum Indicator

Relative Strength Index (RSI)

RSI is a momentum indicator that measures the speed and magnitude of recent price changes. It ranges from 0 to 100.

Common Reference Levels:

0 30 Oversold 70 Overbought 100

Above 70 → Market may be overbought

Below 30 → Market may be oversold

How Traders Use RSI:

  • Identify strong momentum
  • Compare RSI with price to spot divergences
  • Confirm the strength of an existing trend

Beginner Mistake

Immediately selling because RSI is above 70. In a strong uptrend, RSI can stay above 70 for a long time!

Momentum Indicator

MACD (Moving Average Convergence Divergence)

MACD measures momentum by comparing two exponential moving averages.

MACD Consists Of:

  • MACD Line — The difference between two EMAs
  • Signal Line — EMA of the MACD Line
  • Histogram — Shows whether momentum is getting stronger or weaker

Possible Interpretations:

MACD crosses above Signal Line → Possibly increasing bullish momentum

MACD crosses below Signal Line → Possibly increasing bearish momentum

Best Used For:

  • Trend confirmation
  • Momentum analysis
  • Identifying possible trend changes
Trend Indicator

Moving Averages (MA)

Moving averages smooth price data to identify trends.

Simple Moving Average (SMA):

50 SMA = Average closing price over the last 50 periods

Exponential Moving Average (EMA):

EMA gives more weight to recent prices, making it react faster to current market conditions.

Popular EMAs:

20 EMA — Short-term

50 EMA — Medium-term

100 EMA — Longer-term

200 EMA — Long-term

General Interpretation:

Price above MA → Possible uptrend

Price below MA → Possible downtrend

Lagging Indicator

Moving averages are lagging indicators and should never be used alone.

Volume Indicator

Volume

Volume measures how much of an asset was traded.

Examples:

Price rises + Volume rises

Stronger buying interest. The move is more reliable.

Price rises + Volume falls

Less participation. The move may be less sustainable.

High volume = Greater market participation

Low volume = Less conviction

Volatility Indicator

Bollinger Bands

Bollinger Bands consist of three lines that measure market volatility:

  • Upper Band
  • Middle Band (Moving Average)
  • Lower Band

Wide Bands

High volatility

Narrow Bands

Low volatility, sometimes preceding larger price moves

Not an Automatic Signal

Price touching the upper or lower band is not an automatic buy or sell signal.

Momentum Indicator

Stochastic Oscillator

The Stochastic Oscillator compares the closing price to the recent trading range.

Scale: 0-100

0 20 Low 80 High 100

Above 80 → High relative position within recent range

Below 20 → Low relative position within recent range

Often used to assess momentum.

Volatility Indicator

Average True Range (ATR)

ATR measures volatility. It provides no direction.

High ATR

Large price swings

Low ATR

Smaller price swings

Practical Use

Many traders use ATR to set stop-loss distances based on market volatility.

Advanced

Fibonacci Retracement

Fibonacci levels help identify areas where price might pause or reverse during a pullback.

Common Levels:

23.6% — Light retracement

38.2% — Mild retracement

50.0% — Midpoint

61.8% — "Golden Ratio"

78.6% — Deep retracement

Potential Zones

These levels are potential support or resistance zones, not guaranteed turning points.

Volume Indicator

VWAP (Volume Weighted Average Price)

VWAP combines price and volume to calculate the average price traded throughout the day.

Common Interpretations:

Price above VWAP → Buyers are paying above the daily average

Price below VWAP → Sellers are accepting lower than the daily average

Resets Daily

VWAP resets every trading day. Most useful for intraday trading.

Advanced

Ichimoku Cloud

The Ichimoku Cloud combines multiple indicators into one system.

Helps Assess:

  • Trend direction
  • Momentum
  • Potential support and resistance

Complex for Beginners

Although powerful, Ichimoku can seem complex for beginners. Focus on simpler indicators first.

Volume Indicator

On-Balance Volume (OBV)

OBV combines price movement with trading volume.

Purpose:

Determine whether volume generally supports the price trend.

Interpretation:

Price rises + OBV rises → Buying pressure may be increasing

Price rises + OBV falls → Divergence, possibly a weak trend

Trend Indicator

ADX (Average Directional Index)

ADX measures trend strength, not direction.

Scale: 0-100

0-20 → Weak or ranging market

20-25 → Trend starting to form

25-50 → Strong trend

50-100 → Very strong trend

ADX is often combined with other indicators that identify trend direction.

Intermediate

Combining Indicators

Using multiple indicators can provide a more balanced market view.

Example Combination:

Indicator Purpose
200 EMA Long-term trend
RSI Momentum
MACD Momentum shifts
Volume Confirms market participation
ATR Volatility

Avoid Redundancy

Do not use multiple indicators that measure the same thing — they give redundant information.

Common Beginner Mistakes

  • Using only one indicator
  • Ignoring the broader market trend
  • Entering trades without considering volume
  • Believing indicators predict the future
  • Treating every crossover as a trading signal
  • Neglecting risk management
